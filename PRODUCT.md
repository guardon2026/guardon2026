## Users

GuardOn serves three operational user groups in the Korean security staffing market.

Security company owners and dispatch managers use the product when a confirmed job suddenly lacks enough people. They are often under same-day pressure, comparing available workers, credentials, locations, and response status while trying to avoid phone-tree coordination.

Security workers and certified freelancers use the product to keep their profile, credentials, availability, notifications, assignments, and point balance current. Their context is mobile-first or field-adjacent, with short decision windows for accepting or rejecting SOS requests.

Administrators use the product to approve companies, review credentials, monitor SOS failures, and keep the marketplace trustworthy during early operations.

## Product Purpose

GuardOn is a web-first B2B security staffing platform for resolving same-day staffing gaps without manual calls. Its core experience is SOS urgent matching: a company submits a request, nearby available and verified workers are notified, workers accept, and the company confirms assignments.

Success means company users can register and complete their first search within 30 minutes, SOS requests surface viable workers within 5 minutes, and urgent staffing can be confirmed in roughly 8 minutes when the local worker pool is sufficient. For workers, success means a verified profile and availability state lead to real matching opportunities within days, not vague job-board browsing.

## Brand Personality

GuardOn should feel grounded in the field, fast under pressure, and stable enough to trust with real staffing risk.

The working personality is: 현장감, 빠름, 안정감.

Voice is formal Korean, direct, operational, and calm. The interface should sound like a competent dispatcher: clear about state, specific about next action, and never cute or vague.

## Anti-references

Avoid generic startup SaaS styling that makes an urgent field operations product feel like a lightweight marketing template.

Avoid dark-mode-first dashboards, purple or blue-purple gradient aesthetics, glassmorphism, decorative hero metrics, over-rounded cards, and ornamental motion.

Avoid English UI copy, English placeholders, and informal Korean. User-facing service copy must be Korean in 존댓말.

Avoid treating SOS as just another generic primary action. The SOS color and language should remain distinct because it signals urgency and real-world staffing pressure.

## Design Principles

1. Make urgency legible without panic. SOS flows should draw attention immediately, but state, timing, and options must stay calm and readable.
2. Build trust from operational evidence. Credential badges, approval states, match counts, locations, histories, and admin review outcomes should be easy to scan.
3. Keep the product task-first. Company, worker, and admin surfaces should prioritize repeated workflows over decorative storytelling.
4. Respect Korean field context. Copy, density, labels, and navigation should fit Korean B2B operations and mobile-adjacent use.
5. Preserve semantic restraint. Brand blue is for platform identity and primary navigation; SOS red is reserved for urgent SOS CTAs and destructive or emergency states.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA contrast for body text, controls, form labels, table content, and state badges. Do not rely on color alone for SOS status, credential status, approval state, or destructive actions.

Support reduced-motion preferences. Motion should communicate state changes or feedback only, generally within 150-250 ms.

Interfaces must remain usable on mobile widths, with Korean text wrapping cleanly and no button or card text overflow. Placeholder and helper text must remain readable, not pale gray.

Because the service handles personal, location, credential, and work-history information, privacy-sensitive states should be explicit and reassuring without adding friction.
